Nanotech Breakthrough: Spinach as Mine Detectors

  • Nov. 1st, 2016
Share

MIT scientists recently published in the journal Nature Materials a report that scientists can use miniature implantable nanoscale carbon tubes in plants that can detect the surrounding material composition.

Using this method, scientists can transform spinach into mine detectors.

The researchers implant nanoscale carbon tubes into spinach leaves, and then the leaves absorb the nitro aromatics contained in mines and subsurface explosives.

Trials have shown that spinach leaves take about 10 minutes to absorb moisture.

Researchers measure data with a non-contact approach by shining a laser on leaves which excites the leaf, causing the nanotubes to emit near-infrared fluorescent rays.

A small infrared camera can detect fluorescence, and this camera in turn can be connected to a small computer.

This research provides important proof of principal, said participant Professor Strano (Michael Strano) of Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

He said, "Our paper shows we can detect anything using this type method to control plants."

Professor Strano's lab prior to this research had developed nanoscale carbon microtubes capable of detecting hydrogen peroxide, TNT, and sarin nerve gas.

Scientists say luminescence is altered when the target molecules encapsulated in nanoparticles combine with polymer materials surrounding the nanotubes.

Professor Strano said that such use of plants as detectors can be used in matters of national defense, the monitoring of public spaces, and the prevention of terrorist activities.

He said that plants can be used to detect groundwater seepage from explosives or underground waste containing nitro aromatics.

Current technology allows for scientists to capture data within one meter of the plant, and further efforts are being made to lengthen this distance.
